# The 1990s: Split Screen

## Decade Overview

The 1990s are dominated by a split-screen aesthetic: two opposite anti-glamours defined the decade in tension with each other.

**Track 1: Minimalist luxury.** MFIT describes minimalism as a conscious reaction to 1980s excess, intensified by economic recession that made conspicuous wealth display feel tactless. Calvin Klein, Jil Sander, Helmut Lang: clean column silhouettes, high-quality basics, quiet accessories.

**Track 2: Grunge anti-polish.** The Pacific Northwest alternative music scene produced a layered, mismatched, deliberately "tatty" look that became mainstream after Nirvana's 1991 breakthrough. The opposite of minimalism's refinement, but equally opposed to 1980s excess.

Both tracks are "anti-glamour" — they just define glamour's opposite differently.

Subcultures also remained highly visible: Britpop revived mod codes, rave/clubwear remixed retro sportswear, and hip-hop aesthetics achieved mainstream media visibility.

## Key Palette Fork

**Minimalism 90s:** Neutral, white, black, camel, grey — no loudness  
**Grunge:** Plaid flannel earth tones, faded black, dirty white  
**Britpop:** Khaki, navy, black, white — mod palette revival  
**Y2K:** Black, silver, chrome, optional acid brights  
**Hip-hop late 90s:** Bold brights + black/white + logo pops

## Decade Tensions

| Pole A | Pole B |
|--------|--------|
| Minimalism (precision, restraint) | Grunge (layered anti-polish) |
| Britpop retro mod revival | Y2K tech futurism |
| Quiet luxury (no logo) | Hip-hop logo economy |
| Japanese/Belgian deconstruction | Mainstream minimalism |

---

## Decade Inflection Points

- **1991**: Nirvana's Nevermind; grunge goes mainstream
- **1993**: Marc Jacobs' Perry Ellis grunge collection (fired); grunge on the runway
- **1995**: Minimalism consolidates as dominant luxury direction
- **1997**: Britpop at cultural peak; Cool Britannia discourse
- **1998–1999**: Y2K visual language intensifies on runways
