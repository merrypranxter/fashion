# Influence Graph

Style movements from 1960–2000 do not exist in isolation. This document maps the documented influence edges between movements — useful for hybrid prompt generation (e.g., "punk + Japanese deconstruction," "mod revival + Britpop," "grunge + minimalism").

Edges are conservative and anchored in institutional framings of borrowing/impact, drawing especially on the Metropolitan Museum of Art's punk exhibition framing, Museum at FIT's nineties overview, and V&A collection histories.

## Key Influence Clusters

### Youth Underground → High Fashion Pipeline
Street styles repeatedly surface into high fashion through a documented mechanism: subcultural signals get absorbed, translated, and re-sold by couture and ready-to-wear.

- **Punk → runway**: The Met frames punk as an early manifestation of deconstructionist fashion; couture repeatedly borrows punk's visual vocabulary (rips, hardware, safety pins).
- **Grunge → runway**: Marc Jacobs' 1993 Perry Ellis collection translates grunge into luxury; the moment is cited as the first mainstream runway co-optation of the look.
- **Hip-hop → luxury**: By the late 90s, sportswear brands and luxury labels are bidirectionally engaged with hip-hop's logo economy.
- **Club scenes → catwalk**: New Romantic club culture, acid house, and 90s rave aesthetics all produced design vocabulary later visible on runways.

### Hybrid Prompt Suggestions
These combinations are historically grounded (the source styles genuinely influenced each other):

- `punk + deconstruction` → distressed tailoring, anti-convention construction
- `mod + space age` → geometric minimalism + futurist materials
- `hip-hop + preppy` → the Ralph Lauren recontextualization pattern
- `rave + skate` → baggy sportswear, function-first, youth outdoor
- `new romantic + goth` → both emerged from same post-punk London club scene
- `grunge + minimalism` → the 90s split-screen aesthetic
- `Japanese avant-garde + Belgian avant-garde` → concept garment territory
